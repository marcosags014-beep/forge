### SHOW HN POST

---

**Title:** Show HN: FORGE — localStorage-first identity OS that scores your daily alignment across 6 life domains

---

**Body:**

I've been building FORGE for the past few months and wanted to share it with HN because the technical decisions were shaped by this community's writing on data ownership and lightweight architecture.

**What it does:**

FORGE asks you to define your identity across 6 domains (Health, Money, Work, Relationships, Mind, Purpose), then scores your daily actions against that identity as an Alignment Score (0-100). An AI layer called Oracle — powered by Claude — reads cross-domain correlations and surfaces insights that single-domain tracking misses.

**Technical decisions worth discussing:**

*localStorage-first architecture:* No account required to start. All identity data, daily logs, and score history live in localStorage by default. This was a deliberate choice — I wanted zero friction between "I want to try this" and "I have a score." The tradeoff is obvious (no cross-device sync, data loss risk), but it eliminated the signup-drop-off problem completely. Users who want cloud sync opt in.

*6-domain correlation engine:* The interesting engineering problem was building a correlation engine that finds relationships across domains without requiring statistically significant sample sizes. Most users won't have 90 days of data. The algorithm uses weighted moving averages and a Bayesian inference layer to surface likely correlations after as few as 7-10 data points per domain. It shows confidence intervals, not certainties.

*Alignment Score algorithm:* Each domain is scored 0-100 based on logged actions vs. stated identity weights. The composite score is not a simple average — domains are weighted by the user's own stated priorities, so a user who says health is their top value has health contribute more heavily to their overall score. This prevents the gamification problem where users optimize for score rather than growth.

*Oracle prompt architecture:* The AI doesn't get raw logs. It receives a structured summary of score deltas, domain correlations above a confidence threshold, and journal sentiment signals. The prompt is designed to ask one clarifying question rather than deliver advice — this keeps the interaction from becoming a chatbot and forces reflection.

**Body:**

I want to write the post I wish I'd read before I started.

Not a "look how fast we grew" post. A "here's what actually happened and what I learned from each step" post.

**What "activated" means in FORGE**

This matters because activated ≠ signed up. In FORGE, a user is activated when they:

1. Complete identity setup across all 6 domains
2. Log at least 3 days of actions
3. Have an Alignment Score and have opened Oracle at least once

By that definition, 52% of people who complete onboarding become activated users. The other 48% stop at setup or log once and disappear. Understanding that gap has been most of my job.

**The zero-dollar distribution strategy**

I didn't have a budget. I had a Twitter account and a genuine belief that the product solved a real problem. So everything was content-first.

Week 1-2: I posted daily about the identity gap — the psychological concept the whole product is built on. Not about FORGE. About the underlying idea. "You're consistent with who you believe you are, not who you want to be." That tweet got 400 engagements organically. I put a link in the reply.

Week 3-4: I started posting cross-domain data insights from the handful of early users I had. "Sleep under 6.5h correlates with 34% higher impulse spending the next day." These performed better than anything product-related. The lesson: lead with insight, follow with product.

Reddit: I posted in r/productivity, r/selfimprovement, and r/nosurf with honest posts about why I built this and what problem I was trying to solve. No spam, no link in the first comment. Asked for feedback. Got 40+ users from three posts.

Show HN: One submission that got modest traction but brought in technically sophisticated users who gave brutal, useful feedback. Worth it.

**What's working that I didn't expect**

Day-3 email is my highest-leverage touchpoint. Users who open the day-3 email have 3x higher activation rates than those who don't. The email is two paragraphs. No images. Just "day 3 is when Oracle starts making sense — here's why it's worth logging today." Simple. Personal. Effective.

The identity setup itself is a conversion moment. Users who finish setup come back. Users who skip a domain don't. I added a "why this domain matters" tooltip to each one and completion rate went from 61% to 84%.

Oracle honesty builds trust. I trained Oracle to say "I don't have enough data yet" when it genuinely doesn't — instead of hallucinating a pattern. Three users specifically mentioned this in feedback interviews as the reason they trusted the product. An AI that admits uncertainty is unusual enough to be remarkable.

**What's not working**

Cold DMs to creators: 10 sent, 2 responses, 0 trials so far. Need a better hook or a warmer intro path.

Posting product screenshots: minimal engagement. Posting the insight behind the product: 5-10x more engagement. Screenshots are for after people care about you.

Long onboarding explanations: users don't read them. I cut my onboarding copy by 60% and conversion went up. People want to get to their number, not understand the philosophy first.

**The one insight that changed how I think about growth**

Retention is the only metric that matters in the first 90 days. Not signups. Not pageviews. Whether people come back on day 7.

My day-7 retention is 74%. The benchmark for self-improvement apps is around 18%. That gap is the whole business. Because if people come back, they activate. If they activate, they tell someone. If they tell someone, I don't need ads.

I'm not celebrating yet. 300 activated users is a start, not a success. But the number I'm proudest of isn't 300. It's that 74%.
